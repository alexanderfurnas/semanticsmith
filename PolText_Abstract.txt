 Recent work in American Politics using text analysis techniques has discovered patterns of coordination, information sharing and policy diffusion by looking at the speech of Members of Congress (Lin, Margolin, Lazer 20107), introduced federal legislation (Wilkerson, Smith, Stramp 2015), and model state legislation (Hertel-Fernandez & Kashin 2015; Kroeger 2016; Linder, Desmarais, Burgess, Giraudy 2016). This work often relies on a common genomic sequencing algorithms borrowed from biostatistics, the Smith-Waterman local alignment algorithm (Smith & Waterman 1981). This paper details a new method for detecting text reuse and paraphrasing in political texts, a critical measurement task in leveraging new text data to observe patterns of coordination, knowledge uptake, and policy transmission and diffusion. The method proposed is an extension of the Smith-Waterman local alignment algorithm with semantically aware mismatch penalties. This modification enables detection of instances of text reuse in which words are changed to semantically similar alternatives to fit new contexts or disguise the source of the text. Our implementation allows for arbitrary user defined semantic spaces, as well as arbitrary weighting of sematic dimensions. This enables users to tailor the semantic weighting to meet their domain specific needs. In this application we use a semantic spaced derived using DEEP LEARNING BEN EDWARDS TKTK.  We apply this technique to a corpus of congressional committee reports and Congressional Research Service reports. We use the identified instances of text reuse to analyse when and under what conditions Committees staffers rely on in house analysis produced by CRS as they craft policy reports justifying the committees recommendations to the floor. We also analyse how relience on CRS reports relates to a bill's progress through further stages of the legislative process.
